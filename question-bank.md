# UX Interview Guide Builder — Question Bank
Version 2.0 | UX Research Team | Last updated: May 2026

---

This page is the knowledge source for the UX Interview Guide Builder
Copilot agent. Every question is designed to activate episodic memory
— felt experience, not logical opinion.

**Foundational principle:** Good questions do not guarantee good research.
Listening quality determines research quality. A guide is a safety net —
not a script. If a participant opens up on question three, stay there.
The list exists so the moderator never gets lost — not so they get through it.

---

## CORE PRINCIPLE

Ask about specific moments, not general positions.

| Avoid (semantic) | Use (episodic) |
| --- | --- |
| What do you think about X? | Tell me about the last time X happened |
| How do you feel about the process? | Walk me through what happened |
| What challenges do you face? | Tell me about a time it completely fell apart |
| What do you typically do? | What did you do the last time? |

---

## FIVE ACTIVATION TECHNIQUES

Every question must use at least one. Label it in brackets.

1. MOMENT ANCHOR — "the last time", "this week", "yesterday"
  Grounds the participant in a real specific episode, not a general impression.

2. WALK-THROUGH — "take me through what happened"
  Narrative recall activates emotion. Abstract discussion suppresses it.

3. CONTRAST — "a time it worked vs. a time it didn't"
  PRECONDITION: Only use when you can safely assume both poles exist.
  Friction and failure are always safe to assume.
  Success and smoothness are not — never assume a positive experience.
  If unsure, anchor to the real experience and let the participant volunteer the contrast.

4. PROJECTION — "what would your team say?", "if I watched you..."
  Imagining an observer or speaking for others bypasses self-editing.

5. BEHAVIOURAL ANCHOR — "what's the first thing you do when..."
  Behaviour under pressure reveals real coping. Avoidance is data.

## LEADING QUESTION RULES

Run all three passes on every question before use.

### Pass 1 — Loaded Emotion Words
Remove these from any question — they belong in the participant's mouth, not yours:
frustrated · hate · love · excited · dread · annoyed · difficult · easy
confusing · terrible · great · broken · boring · painful · significant
gap · error · problem · struggle · hardest

### Pass 2 — Leading Patterns

| Pattern | Wrong | Right |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Yes/no opener | Have you ever experienced X? | Tell me about a time X happened |
| Anchored options | Is it frustrating or fine? | How does it feel? |
| Double-barrelled | What do you like AND dislike? | Split into two questions |
| Accusatory why | Why did you do that? | What led to that? |
| Embedded assumption | What's hard about X under time pressure? | What makes X hard? |

### Pass 3 — Contrast Precondition
Before every CONTRAST question ask: am I certain both poles exist?
If not — drop the positive pole. Anchor to the real experience only.

## WARM-UP QUESTIONS
Goal: open a narrative door. Never ask a yes/no question in warm-up.

### W1
Question: Take me back to this morning — or your most recent shift. What did the first hour look like?
Technique: MOMENT ANCHOR
Timing: ~4 min
Reanchor probe: Take me to the very first moment — what was the first thing you picked up or opened?
Deepen probe: You mentioned [X] — stay with that. What does that look like in practice?
Listen for: Pace of day, staffing context, their role in team — frames everything that follows

### W2
Question: Tell me about your role — not the job description version, the "this is what my day actually looks like" version.
Technique: CONTRAST — role vs. reality
Timing: ~4 min
Reanchor probe: Walk me through what yesterday actually looked like.
Deepen probe: You said [X] — is that how it's supposed to work, or something you figured out?
Listen for: Gap between official role and actual day — normalised workarounds surface here

### W3
Question: Tell me about a week at work recently that's still on your mind — one that didn't quite leave you when you went home. What made it stick?
Technique: MOMENT ANCHOR
Timing: ~4 min
Reanchor probe: Take me back to a specific moment in that week — what was happening?
Deepen probe: What was it about that week that made it stay with you?
Listen for: Their own emotional language — let them set the benchmark, not us

### W4
Question: Tell me about something you've figured out at work that no one taught you — something you just had to work out yourself.
Technique: PROJECTION from self
Timing: ~3 min
Reanchor probe: When did you figure that out — what made you realise you needed to?
Deepen probe: Is there anyone on your team who does it differently?
Listen for: Workarounds and adaptations so normalised they're invisible — richest design signals

### W5
Question: What's the thing on your plate right now that you keep putting off? Why does it keep getting pushed?
Technique: BEHAVIOURAL ANCHOR — avoidance
Timing: ~3 min
Reanchor probe: When did you last put it off — what did you do instead?
Deepen probe: What would need to be true for you to stop putting it off?
Listen for: Avoidance reveals the highest friction — the most avoided task is the most painful one

## DIGGING IN — Behaviour and Context
Never ask what they "typically" do. Typical = constructed. Last time = real.

### D1
Question: Walk me through the last time you did [X activity] — start from the moment you knew you had to do it.
Technique: MOMENT ANCHOR + WALK-THROUGH
Timing: ~6 min
Reanchor probe: Take me back to that specific moment — what was the first thing you picked up or opened?
Deepen probe: And then what happened — walk me through the next step.
Listen for: Specific tools named, actual sequence, who else was involved, hesitations in the telling

### D2
Question: Tell me about a time [X activity] went sideways. What happened?
Technique: MOMENT ANCHOR
Timing: ~5 min
Reanchor probe: Take me back to that moment — what was the first sign it was going wrong?
Deepen probe: What did you do with that — how did you handle it in the moment?
Listen for: Coping behaviours under pressure, who they turn to, what they sacrifice to recover

### D3
Question: What's the first thing you do when [X activity] starts going wrong?
Technique: BEHAVIOURAL ANCHOR
Timing: ~4 min
Reanchor probe: Tell me about the last time that happened — what did you actually do?
Deepen probe: And if that hadn't worked — then what?
Listen for: Escalation path and backup behaviours — reveals system brittleness

### D4
Question: If I followed you around and watched you doing [X activity], what would I see that would probably surprise me?
Technique: PROJECTION
Timing: ~4 min
Reanchor probe: When did that last happen — walk me through it.
Deepen probe: Why would that surprise someone — is it something you've just accepted as normal?
Listen for: Normalised workarounds they've stopped noticing — these are the richest design signals

### D5
Question: How much of your week does [X activity] actually take up — not what it's supposed to take, what it actually takes?
Technique: CONTRAST — supposed to vs. actually
Timing: ~3 min
Reanchor probe: What makes it longer on the heavy weeks?
Deepen probe: If that time disappeared — what would you do with it?
Listen for: Time as a pain proxy and the emotional weight of that cost

### D6
Question: Tell me about a time [X activity] left you feeling like you'd done it properly. What made that possible?
Technique: CONTRAST — PRECONDITION: only use after participant confirms positive experience exists
Timing: ~4 min
Reanchor probe: When was the last time that happened — what specifically made it different?
Deepen probe: What would need to be in place more often for that to happen regularly?
Listen for: Their own definition of good — never assume this exists, only ask if they've volunteered it

## SYSTEMS AND ORGANISATIONAL DYNAMICS
Goal: surface the people, processes, approvals, handoffs, and invisible labour that shape
individual experience. Real work happens in systems — not just between one user and one task.
Use these questions when research reveals that friction comes from coordination, not just interface.

### SO1
Question: Walk me through who else gets involved when [X activity] gets complicated.
Technique: WALK-THROUGH — system mapping
Timing: ~5 min
Reanchor probe: Tell me about the last time that happened — who did you contact first?
Deepen probe: What did you expect from them, and what actually happened?
Listen for: Handoff points, waiting periods, unspoken expectations between roles

### SO2
Question: Tell me about the last time you had to wait on someone else before you could move forward with [X activity].
Technique: MOMENT ANCHOR — dependency
Timing: ~5 min
Reanchor probe: What were you waiting for exactly — what was the blocker?
Deepen probe: What did you do while you waited — how did you manage that time?
Listen for: Hidden dependencies, coordination costs, workarounds built around waiting

### SO3
Question: Tell me about a time [X activity] got held up by an approval or a sign-off. What happened?
Technique: MOMENT ANCHOR — approval friction
Timing: ~4 min
Reanchor probe: Who did the approval go to — and what did that process look like?
Deepen probe: What would have happened if you'd skipped it?
Listen for: Where authority sits, where trust breaks down, what approvals are protecting

### SO4
Question: If I asked your manager to describe how [X activity] works, what would they say — and how would that differ from reality?
Technique: PROJECTION — organisational perception gap
Timing: ~4 min
Reanchor probe: Tell me about a time where that gap caused a problem.
Deepen probe: Have you ever tried to explain the difference — what happened?
Listen for: Distance between how leadership thinks work happens and how it actually happens

### SO5
Question: Tell me about the part of [X activity] that nobody officially owns — the part that just falls to whoever's available.
Technique: MOMENT ANCHOR — invisible labour
Timing: ~4 min
Reanchor probe: When did that last land on you — walk me through it.
Deepen probe: Is that something anyone knows about, or does it just happen quietly?
Listen for: Unacknowledged work, invisible coordination labour, the things that fall through organisational cracks

## POSITIVE SIGNAL DISCOVERY
Goal: surface competence, trust, flow, and relief — not just friction.
Research that only finds what is broken produces diagnostically strong but strategically incomplete findings.
Strong experiences also amplify confidence, momentum, and identity. Balance friction discovery with signal discovery.

PRECONDITION: These questions require the participant to have experienced positive states.
Do not use them speculatively. Only introduce them once the participant has volunteered a positive reference,
or after sufficient rapport that a positive recall feels safe.

### PS1
Question: Tell me about a moment when [X activity] felt effortless — where you finished and thought "that actually worked."
Technique: MOMENT ANCHOR — positive episode
Timing: ~4 min
Reanchor probe: Take me back to that specific moment — what was different about that day?
Deepen probe: What made it possible — was it something you did, or something that was in place?
Listen for: Enablers the participant names — these are design signals as valuable as blockers

### PS2
Question: When does [X activity] feel like it's working well enough that you stop thinking about it?
Technique: BEHAVIOURAL ANCHOR — flow state
Timing: ~4 min
Reanchor probe: Tell me about the last time that happened — when was it?
Deepen probe: What had to be true for that to happen?
Listen for: Conditions that enable flow — often invisible because they're working

### PS3
Question: Tell me about a part of this work you feel you've genuinely mastered — something you're good at that others aren't.
Technique: PROJECTION from self — competence
Timing: ~3 min
Reanchor probe: When did you realise you'd figured that out?
Deepen probe: What does that look like in practice — what do you do differently?
Listen for: Tacit expertise and competence signals — reveals what the system does well when working

### PS4
Question: Tell me about a time this process gave you a result you were proud of. What happened?
Technique: MOMENT ANCHOR — meaningful outcome
Timing: ~4 min
Reanchor probe: Walk me through that — what did you do that made it work?
Deepen probe: What did that feel like when it came together?
Listen for: The emotional relief and identity signals in positive outcomes — "proud" is a strong trust indicator

### PS5
Question: What's the one part of [X activity] that you'd fight to keep if everything else changed?
Technique: CONTRAST — value under change
Timing: ~3 min
Reanchor probe: Tell me about a time that part came through for you — what happened?
Deepen probe: What would break if that disappeared?
Listen for: Core value propositions in the participant's own words — what they'd protect is what they truly value

## CONTINUOUS CUSTOMER — Existing Product Users
Goal: understand lived experience — not their rating of the product, but what it feels like to use it.

### C1
Question: Take me back to the last time you opened the product. What were you trying to do?
Technique: MOMENT ANCHOR
Timing: ~5 min
Reanchor probe: Take me to the very moment you opened it — what made you reach for it then?
Deepen probe: And what happened — did it go the way you expected?
Listen for: Actual use cases vs. intended ones — where the product fits in their real workflow

### C2
Question: Tell me about a moment where you thought "this isn't quite right" and stepped away from it.
Technique: MOMENT ANCHOR
Timing: ~5 min
Reanchor probe: Take me back to that specific moment — what were you trying to do?
Deepen probe: What did you do instead — how did you finish what you were trying to do?
Listen for: Real friction and shadow tools — the workaround they use outside the product

### C3
Question: What's the thing you do in the product that you've never quite trusted — where you finish and think "I hope that worked"?
Technique: BEHAVIOURAL ANCHOR — uncertainty in use
Timing: ~4 min
Reanchor probe: Tell me about the last time you had that feeling — what had you just done?
Deepen probe: What do you do to check — or do you just hope?
Listen for: Uncertainty reveals low-confidence features and missing feedback signals

### C4
Question: Tell me about the last time you ended up outside the product — in a spreadsheet, email, or Post-it — to finish something you'd started in it.
Technique: MOMENT ANCHOR — shadow tool usage
Timing: ~5 min
Reanchor probe: Walk me through that — what were you trying to do that sent you somewhere else?
Deepen probe: Is that something you do regularly, or was that a one-off?
Listen for: Shadow tool usage precisely surfaces unmet needs — frequency tells you severity

### C5
Question: Have there been moments where you've looked around at other tools? Tell me what prompted that.
Technique: MOMENT ANCHOR — churn signal
Timing: ~5 min
Reanchor probe: When did that happen — what was going on at work at that time?
Deepen probe: How close did you come to actually switching?
Listen for: Churn risk — competitor names, specific unmet needs, proximity to switching

### C6
Question: If the product disappeared tomorrow and you had to explain to your manager why work slowed down, what would you say?
Technique: PROJECTION — forces articulation of real value
Timing: ~4 min
Reanchor probe: What would be the first thing that broke down?
Deepen probe: Is there anything else that could fill that gap?
Listen for: What they actually value — often different from what the team thinks they value

## PRODUCT / MARKET FIT
Goal: find out whether the problem is real and felt. Hypothetical questions kill PMF research.

### P1
Question: Tell me about the last time [X problem] cost you real time or effort. Walk me through what happened.
Technique: MOMENT ANCHOR + WALK-THROUGH
Timing: ~6 min
Reanchor probe: Take me back to that moment — what were you doing when it hit?
Deepen probe: What did you do with that — how did you handle it?
Listen for: Concrete cost of the problem in their words — time, effort, emotional weight

### P2
Question: Take me back to the last time you tried to solve [X problem]. What did you do first?
Technique: MOMENT ANCHOR
Timing: ~5 min
Reanchor probe: Walk me through each step — what did you reach for?
Deepen probe: What made you try that first — had you tried something else before?
Listen for: Actual solution behaviour — the full picture of what they've already tried

### P3
Question: Tell me about the tool or approach that came closest to solving [X problem] — what did it get right, and where did it leave you on your own?
Technique: CONTRAST — closest vs. gap
Timing: ~5 min
Reanchor probe: When did you use it last — what were you trying to do?
Deepen probe: What made you keep looking after it — what was still missing?
Listen for: The gap between available solutions and actual need — this is the product brief

### P4
Question: Walk me through your current process for [X task] — every step, including the ones you're not proud of.
Technique: WALK-THROUGH — permission to be honest
Timing: ~6 min
Reanchor probe: Take me through the very first step — what do you open or pick up first?
Deepen probe: You mentioned [X step] — is that how it's supposed to work, or something you figured out?
Listen for: Manual hacks and workarounds they're embarrassed to admit — often the most important data

### P5
Question: What part of [X process] do you tend to put off until the last possible moment?
Technique: BEHAVIOURAL ANCHOR — avoidance
Timing: ~4 min
Reanchor probe: Tell me about the last time you put it off — what happened when you finally had to do it?
Deepen probe: What would need to change for you to stop putting it off?
Listen for: Avoidance surfaces highest-friction step — not necessarily the most talked-about one

### PMF Screener — Superhuman Method (existing users only)
Question: If you could no longer use our product starting tomorrow, how would that land? Walk me through your first reaction.
Follow-ups:
- What type of person on your team would feel that most?
- What does the product do for you that nothing else does?
- If you were redesigning it from scratch for yourself — what would you keep, what would you cut?

## USABILITY — Prototype and Product Evaluation
Goal: cognitive and emotional experience of use — what it felt like to navigate, not opinion about design.

### U1
Question: Before you touch it — just look at it for a moment. What's your first instinct about what this does?
Technique: MOMENT ANCHOR — first impression before construction
Timing: ~3 min
Reanchor probe: What on the screen made you think that?
Deepen probe: What were you expecting to see that you don't?
Listen for: Genuine first impression — captures real mental model before they start building an opinion

### U2
Question: Tell me about the moment just now where you paused. What was going through your mind?
Technique: MOMENT ANCHOR — pins to observed moment
Timing: ~3 min
Reanchor probe: Take me back to that exact moment — what were you trying to do?
Deepen probe: What did you expect to happen when you got there?
Listen for: The hesitation is the data — understanding the thought behind the pause

### U3
Question: If you'd arrived here on your own — without me in the room — what would you have done at that point?
Technique: PROJECTION — removes observer effect
Timing: ~3 min
Reanchor probe: And if that hadn't worked — what then?
Deepen probe: Have you been in a similar situation with another tool — what did you do there?
Listen for: Real behaviour without observer pressure — what they would actually do alone

### U4
Question: Tell me about the moment where you felt most on your own — where you weren't sure what to do next.
Technique: MOMENT ANCHOR — highest friction point
Timing: ~4 min
Reanchor probe: Take me back to that moment — what were you trying to do?
Deepen probe: What did you look for to get your bearings?
Listen for: Exact location and cause of highest friction — named by participant, not inferred

### U5
Question: If this was a person, how would you describe their personality to someone who'd never met them?
Technique: PROJECTION / METAPHOR — bypasses analytical mode
Timing: ~3 min
Reanchor probe: What would you say they're good at? What would you warn someone about?
Deepen probe: What kind of person would get on well with them?
Listen for: Product personality in their language — reveals brand, trust, emotional relationship

## STANDARD CLOSING — All Session Types
Always use all three. Hold 8 seconds of silence before any prompt on CL1.

### CL1
Question: We've covered a lot — is there something you kept thinking about during our conversation that we didn't quite get to?
Timing: ~3 min
Note: Hold silence for 8 full seconds before prompting. The thing they self-edited during the session often lives here — the most honest thing they say.

### CL2
Question: If you could make sure the people building this understood one thing about your day, what would it be?
Technique: PROJECTION — frames researcher as advocate
Timing: ~3 min
Note: Lowers guard by positioning researcher as ally. Their single most important signal.

### CL3
Question: Is there someone on your team who'd have a completely different take on this than you do? Who should I talk to?
Technique: PROJECTION — generates next participants
Timing: ~2 min
Note: Surfaces next participants and systemic disagreements about the problem.

## MODERATOR PRINCIPLES

### During the session — emotional activation
When abstract answer received: Anchor to specific episode — "Tell me about a specific time that happened — when was the last time?"
When short answer received: Hold silence for 8 full seconds before probing. Silence activates elaboration more reliably than a question.
When interesting word used: Reflect it back — "You said [word] — tell me more about that." Never substitute your word for theirs.
When participant generalises ("we always..."): Ask for an instance — "When did that happen most recently?"
When answer sounds rehearsed: Ask for the rough version — "What does it look like when it's not going that smoothly?"

### Use with care — not hard rules
- Avoid "good", "great", or "interesting" — they signal approval and condition answers. Use "mm-hmm" or silence instead.
- Avoid leading with "why" before the episode is established — it pulls the participant into justification rather than memory. Once a specific moment is grounded, "what led you to that?" is fine.
- Be cautious with hypotheticals about the future — "what would you do if..." invites imagination, not memory. Prefer past behaviour: "what did you do the last time that happened?" There are exceptions — the PMF screener uses a hypothetical deliberately because the emotional reaction is the data.
- Resist rescuing a struggling participant — confusion and hesitation are often the most valuable moments in a session. Note what triggered the struggle and move on only when it's clear the participant is genuinely stuck.
- Wait for the participant's emotional words before using your own — reflect their language back, never substitute yours.

### Contradiction handling
Participants frequently contradict themselves — not from dishonesty, but because behaviour varies by context. Contradictions are data, not errors.

When you notice a contradiction:
- Do not correct or challenge directly — that triggers defensiveness
- Revisit it with curiosity: "Earlier you mentioned [X], and just now you described [Y] — help me understand when each happens."
- Probe the context difference: "What makes it [X] sometimes and [Y] other times?"
- Listen for: situational behaviour — the same person behaves differently under different conditions. Both are true. Both matter.

Example:
Participant says at minute 10: "I use it every day."
Participant says at minute 35: "I avoid it whenever I can."
Probe: "You mentioned earlier you use it every day — and just now that you avoid it when you can. Tell me more about that — when does each happen?"

## EVIDENCE QUALITY

Not all data collected in an interview is equally valuable. Before reporting findings,
assess the quality of each piece of evidence using these dimensions.

### Strong vs weak evidence

| Weak evidence | Strong evidence |
| --- | --- |
| "The workflow is annoying." | "I export everything to Excel every Friday because I don't trust the totals." |
| "It can be slow sometimes." | "Last Tuesday I waited 20 minutes for the report to load — I went and made coffee." |
| "People find it confusing." | "My colleague called me twice last week to ask how to find the export button." |
| "We don't really use that feature." | "I turned it off in week two and haven't touched it since." |
| "It's fine for most things." | "I use it for invoicing but I still do estimates in a spreadsheet." |

Strong evidence is specific, behavioural, and time-anchored.
Weak evidence is general, evaluative, and abstract.
If a finding can only be supported by weak evidence, flag it — don't report it as a validated insight.

### Evidence dimensions — assess each finding against these

| Dimension | Question to ask |
| --- | --- |
| Frequency | How many participants mentioned this — independently, in their own words? |
| Specificity | Is it grounded in a named moment, tool, or behaviour — or is it a general opinion? |
| Emotional weight | How strongly did the participant react — did they return to it unprompted? |
| Workaround cost | What extra effort does the participant invest to cope with this? |
| Corroboration | Does this match what other participants described, or is it a single vivid anecdote? |
| Behavioural evidence | Did you observe this behaviour, or only hear about it? Observed > reported. |

### The vivid anecdote trap
A single dramatic story from one participant can dominate analysis if it is not checked against frequency.
Before including any finding, ask: is this vivid, or is this frequent?
Vivid but rare → worth noting, not prioritising.
Frequent but mundane → always worth prioritising.
Both vivid and frequent → highest confidence finding.

## PLACEHOLDER REFERENCE

Before building any guide, resolve these for the specific participant:

[X activity] = the specific activity being studied
[X problem] = the specific problem being investigated
[X task] = the specific task being examined

Example:
Research: NHS nurses managing patient handover notes
[X activity] = managing patient handover notes at shift changes
[X problem] = keeping handover information complete under time pressure
[X task] = completing a handover at the end of a shift
